# Benchmark Recommendations — New Haven Salt Storage (Draft v1)

## Objective
Recommend practical salt-storage pathway(s) for New Haven that reduce compliance and environmental risk while preserving winter operations.

## Decision options

### Option A — Compliance Hardening at Current Location (Near-term)
**What it is**
- Keep current location for now.
- Upgrade to stronger controls: full cover where feasible, impervious pad integrity, runoff diversion/collection, stricter SOPs/inspection cadence, documented housekeeping and spill response.

**Pros**
- Fastest to implement.
- Lowest near-term disruption to operations.
- Lower immediate capex than relocation.

**Cons / risks**
- Residual shoreline exposure remains.
- Community/public acceptance risk remains elevated.
- Could become insufficient if enforcement posture tightens.

**Best use**
- As a stopgap while designing medium/long-term transition.

---

### Option B — Hybrid Model (Port Transload + Inland Municipal Storage)
**What it is**
- Keep maritime delivery at/near port facilities.
- Move day-to-day municipal inventory to inland covered storage.
- Use truck transfer from port to inland municipal yards.

**Pros**
- Reduces direct waterfront storage exposure.
- Preserves logistics advantages of marine delivery.
- Matches peer pattern seen in Duluth/Green Bay-Howard case framing.

**Cons / risks**
- Requires inter-site coordination and transfer logistics.
- Added operating complexity (two-node model).
- Requires careful contracting/interface definition with terminal operators.

**Best use**
- Medium-term target balancing feasibility and risk reduction.

---

### Option C — Full Inland Municipal Storage (Long-term)
**What it is**
- Shift municipal salt storage fully inland in enclosed, engineered facilities.
- Waterfront role limited to logistics transload (or reduced reliance over time).

**Pros**
- Strongest long-term risk reduction for runoff/community concerns.
- Cleaner public narrative and land-use alignment.

**Cons / risks**
- Highest capex and longest timeline.
- Siting/permitting complexity.
- Potential logistics cost increases depending on distance and throughput.

**Best use**
- Long-term endpoint if city wants maximum risk reduction and redevelopment alignment.

---

## Recommended path (working recommendation)

**Recommended sequence: A → B, while evaluating C as strategic endpoint.**

1. **Immediate (0–6 months):** execute Option A controls + audit trail.
2. **Medium term (6–24 months):** design and launch Option B operating model.
3. **Strategic (24+ months):** decide whether to mature B or transition to C based on cost, siting feasibility, and policy direction.
