Salt Stockpiles on the Mill River Waterfront in New Haven: Current State, Risks, and Relocation Options
Executive summary and key findings
Large bulk road salt stockpiles are stored at and around the industrial property at 347 Chapel Street and 259 East Street along the Mill River waterfront in New Haven, adjacent to residential areas and sensitive community assets. The site sits directly next to the river and is described by commenters and the state as being, at least in part, within a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area and within an Environmental Justice community. 
[1]
Ownership and operations are split across entities. Southern Connecticut Gas Company owns at least one parcel at 347 Chapel Street and has leased it for bulk materials and salt storage since 1998, while Gateway Terminal, operated as part of Enstructure’s Connecticut network, has been identified in state and local reporting as the salt pile operator under the industrial stormwater permitting program. 
[2]
The most important data gaps for decision making are the maximum on site salt inventory (tons), annual throughput (tons per year), stormwater discharge configuration, and measured chloride or conductivity gradients in the receiving waters near the facility. Even the public record that raised the issue explicitly describes limited long term monitoring data for the Mill River at the relevant locations. 
[3]
Regulatory context is unusually salient because Connecticut’s 2025 Industrial Stormwater General Permit (CTR050000) has explicit sector specific requirements for bulk solid de icing materials storage at or above 30,000 tons capacity, including detailed tarp specifications, sweeping cadence, wheel wash recommendations, and requirements to keep salt covered outside of active loading and unloading. That permit simultaneously contains an exemption for facilities at or above 30,000 tons from mandatory structural enclosure, which is a core policy tension driving calls for relocation or permanent covered storage. 
[4]
From a consulting perspective, the relocation question is not only an environmental compliance issue. It is also a logistics and regional resiliency problem because salt is a major maritime import at the Port of New Haven, and Gateway Terminal is identified by the US Army Corps of Engineers as having built salt storage tied to salt imports (including imports through Morton Salt). Any relocation that breaks ship rail truck connectivity risks adding significant system cost or shifting impacts to other communities. 
[5]
Across comparable states, the strongest pattern is that compliance and 
community acceptance improve materially when agencies move from uncovered piles to covered storage on impermeable pads
 with runoff capture and controlled load out, and when siting avoids floodplains and drinking water protection zones. The most concrete project level evidence in the retrieved record includes Massachusetts and Wisconsin municipal and 
DOT examples that couple new or relocated yards with salt sheds and stormwater controls, plus a Maine example of relocating a fabric salt shed at a fraction of replacement cost. 
[6]
Current locations, ownership, volumes, and operations
What is on the Mill River waterfront today
The core waterfront salt storage footprint discussed in the public record is located at or near the intersection of Chapel Street and East Street along the Mill River shoreline. This location is repeatedly described as the “facility located at 347 Chapel Street in New Haven” and as being “directly on the edge of the Mill River.” 
[1]
A separate planning document for the Mill River district also describes a “large salt pile storage bounded by East Street, Chapel Street, and the Mill River,” which is consistent with the 347 Chapel Street site geometry described in utility remediation materials. 
[7]
Ownership and land use context
The former manufactured gas plant property is described by United Illuminating’s remediation materials as a 14.6 acre site comprised of two parcels, with Southern Connecticut Gas Company owning 347 Chapel Street and leasing it for “bulk storage of salt and aggregate supplies and materials,” with the other parcel at 259 East Street. 
[8]
The same remediation record ties the shoreline to legacy coal tar residuals and a Connecticut DEEP consent order process, including investigations that include soil, groundwater, surface water, and sediment sampling in and along the river adjacent to the site. This matters because heavy equipment traffic, salt handling, and stormwater conveyance all interact with an already complex environmental baseline. 
[8]
Operator and supply chain role
Within the Connecticut industrial stormwater permitting process, the salt stockpiles at 347 Chapel Street are described as “Gateway Terminal salt piles along the Mill River,” with state acknowledgement that DEEP has taken enforcement action for noncompliance at the facility. 
[9]
Corporate sources indicate Gateway Terminal formed a partnership with Enstructure that covered New Haven assets, and that Enstructure’s Northeast operations are centered on New Haven with ship, barge, truck, and rail capabilities. 
[10]
The US Army Corps of Engineers economic appendix for the New Haven Harbor navigation improvement project states that salt is a major foreign import at the port, used mostly by municipalities and also by commercial retailers, and explicitly notes “Gateway terminal built a storage facility for salt imports from Chile through Morton Salt.” 
[11]
Volumes and throughput
The public regulatory record provides a hard threshold but not a verified maximum.
The relevant regulatory classification is bulk solid de icing material storage with capacity to store 30,000 tons or more at any one time, which is the definition used for Sector AE in Connecticut’s 2025 Industrial Stormwater General Permit. 
[12]
Local reporting about the facility describes “tens of thousands of tons of salt,” references a 2015 shipment “over 30,000 tons,” and describes a spill and exposure scenario where the operator said it was receiving “two additional large shipments of approximately 100,000 tons of de icing materials,” resulting in a period of weeks with salt uncovered. These figures are not confirmed in the permit document itself and should be treated as unverified until reconciled against inventory records and shipping manifests. 
[13]
What is explicitly unspecified in the retrieved official record:
Current maximum on site salt inventory (tons at peak), average end of season inventory (tons), annual throughput (tons per year), and the split between west side and east side stockpiles referenced by commenters are not specified in accessible DEEP documents retrieved here. 
[14]
Recommended data collection methods to close the gap:
A pragmatic approach is a three layer inventory framework.
First, require monthly stockpile mass balance reporting from the operator during the de icing season, including receipts, load outs, and ending inventory, aligned to SWPPP documentation requirements for salt operations and monitoring strategies. 
[15]
Second, validate physical volumes with periodic LiDAR or photogrammetry surveys. A recent DOT oriented research paper describes using portable LiDAR to measure salt stockpile inventory at scale, producing volumetric inventories quickly with limited staffing, which is directly transferable as a verification method. 
[16]
Third, triangulate with maritime import and rail or truck dispatch records, because salt imports via New Haven are significant and port level tonnage data already exist for foreign salt imports at the harbor level even if not facility specific. 
[5]
Environmental impacts and regulatory context in Connecticut
Environmental pathways relevant to the Mill River waterfront
Water quality and conductivity
Connecticut DEEP explains that excess salts can be transported via stormwater into rivers and groundwater, that chloride moves freely through soils and can persist for many years, and that the state has adopted chloride criteria for surface waters of 860 mg/L acute and 230 mg/L chronic. 
[17]
In the same DEEP technical narrative, the agency reports statewide stream chloride averages more than tripled from 14.2 mg/L (2001 to 2005) to 49.7 mg/L (2016 to 2020), and notes emerging research suggesting even small increases can negatively affect stream ecosystems. 
[17]
The salt pile specific concern is runoff and leaching into the Mill River. The hearing officer record documents repeated alarms about siting and maintenance of the 347 Chapel Street stockpiles on the river edge, intermittent tarp coverage, and fear that stormwater runoff is discharging “substantial amounts of salt and other pollutants” into the Mill River, with DEEP acknowledging the potential impacts and stating it has taken enforcement action for noncompliance. 
[18]
Sediment and legacy contamination interactions
The Chapel Street waterfront is also tied to a former manufactured gas plant site with documented coal tar residuals and sheen control via booms, under an active consent order process that includes sediment sampling and shoreline work. Salt handling and heavy equipment movement can aggravate shoreline erosion or disturb compromised stormwater infrastructure, which is important because the public record includes references to oil sheens and erosion concerns alongside salt issues. 
[19]
Groundwater and drinking water risk framing
DEEP emphasizes that chloride can remain in groundwater for years or decades and that remediation generally relies on dilution or costly treatment like reverse osmosis, and that Connecticut has a drinking water maximum contaminant level for chloride of 250 mg/L and a sodium guidance level of 100 mg/L. 
[20]
Air quality and dust
The public hearing record includes community claims about aerosolized salt and particulate matter impacts and positions the facility in an air quality nonattainment context, but these statements are presented as public comment and not verified in DEEP’s technical response. They should be treated as hypotheses to be evaluated via monitoring rather than established facts for this specific site. 
[14]
Stormwater management and flood risks
The site context matters because public commenters and DEEP identify the facility as at least partly in a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area, and because salt storage regulations in the industrial stormwater general permit include constraints for new facilities in floodplains. 
[21]
Connecticut regulatory and permitting framework
Core permit vehicle: CT Industrial Stormwater General Permit CTR050000
Connecticut DEEP’s 2025 Industrial Stormwater General Permit was issued October 1, 2025 and includes a dedicated sector, Sector AE, for bulk solid de icing material storage facilities with capacity to store 30,000 tons or more at any one time. 
[22]
The permit is unusually prescriptive about operational controls that directly match the failure modes described in community complaints.
Key Sector AE requirements relevant to the New Haven waterfront include.
A prohibition framing where “contaminated run off from bulk storage of de icing products is a prohibited non stormwater discharge.” 
[23]
Minimize exposure requirements that road salt be securely covered during the off season and during the on season unless actively loading or unloading, with instructions to minimize exposure during active use using partial covers, roofed structures, or tarps during non active periods. 
[24]
An explicit exemption for facilities at or above 30,000 tons from mandatory structural cover, conditioned on implementing minimum BMPs in the SWPPP, which is central for evaluating whether “build a dome” is a regulatory must or a policy choice. 
[25]
Minimum BMPs that include locating stockpiles on a well maintained paved impermeable surface, maintaining setbacks from property boundaries, and detailed impermeable cover specifications including polyethylene material weight and thickness plus installation, ballasting, and inspection requirements. 
[26]
Operational housekeeping and tracking controls including sweeping of entrances and exits at least twice per week, truck idling restrictions, and wheel wash stations to minimize tracking of material off site. 
[27]
Critical siting constraints embedded in the permit
The general permit section on solid de icing material storage includes specific siting constraints for new salt storage facilities, stating no new road salt or de icing materials storage facilities must be located if the site is within a 100 year floodplain, within 250 feet of a potable well, or within a Level A aquifer protection area. 
[28]
This has two important implications.
Relocation planning must explicitly screen candidate sites against floodplain mapping and drinking water protection overlays early, otherwise a site can fail late in permitting.
If policymakers are considering relocation out of an SFHA, they can credibly frame it as aligning future facility decisions with the constraints already embedded in the state’s stormwater control expectations for new facilities. 
[29]
Environmental Justice and public participation tension
The hearing officer record captures a policy conflict: commenters argue that because the facility is covered by a general permit rather than an individual permit, it has not been subject to certain EJ public participation requirements, while DEEP acknowledges EJ concerns but positions siting and land use beyond the scope of the general permit. This boundary is important when designing a relocation roadmap that is likely to be politically viable. 
[9]
Other Connecticut programs intersecting relocation choices
Aquifer protection
Connecticut’s aquifer protection program regulates land uses that use or store hazardous materials within designated aquifer protection areas to protect major public supply wells, with DEEP responsible for standards and with municipalities playing a critical role in local land use regulation. 
[30]
Within Connecticut aquifer protection regulations, hazardous materials storage above ground within an aquifer protection area is generally constrained to covered storage or storage under a roof with additional containment requirements, reinforcing a strong statewide policy direction toward covered and contained practices where drinking water risk exists. 
[31]
Benchmark case studies in Minnesota, Wisconsin, Massachusetts, New York, and Maine
How to read the benchmark set
The cases below intentionally include three types of precedent because New Haven’s problem can be solved in more than one way.
Hard engineering upgrades at an existing yard.
Relocation or consolidation of a depot with new salt shed construction.
Modular or fabric structures that enable relatively fast relocation at lower cost than replacement.
Because permitting and cost are highly site specific, the benchmarks are best used as anchors for order of magnitude ranges and for what “good” operational controls look like.

Key case study lessons learned that transfer directly to New Haven
Wisconsin municipal shed projects show cost structure composition. The building itself can be a minority of total capital once stormwater management and site work are included, and the permitting narrative often centers on avoiding expensive offsite storage and reducing shortage risk, which is an operational and public safety rationale that resonates with winter maintenance stakeholders. 
[32]
Massachusetts DOT relocation cases show that salt storage and brine tanks are often embedded within a broader maintenance depot move triggered by flooding and stormwater compliance, suggesting New Haven could bundle salt relocation with adjacent port or freight modernization initiatives rather than treating it as a standalone environmental project. 
[37]
Maine demonstrates a regulated approach where siting distance from wells and aquifers and operational covering requirements are explicit and enforceable, which materially reduces ambiguity in what constitutes adequate stewardship. The Maine rule text also uses time based rules for how long the working face can remain uncovered, which provides a concrete operational standard that can be used as a best practice even where Connecticut law differs. 
[35]
Modular relocation in Maine shows a pathway for reducing sunk cost risk. If New Haven wants to de risk relocation by staging a temporary inland covered facility first, fabric structures can be relocated later at a fraction of replacement cost, at least in the vendor reported example.

Core stakeholder conflict to manage
A relocation or major redesign can be framed as a net public benefit but still fail if it shifts cost and risk onto actors who do not have the funding authority to absorb it.
Operators will rationally resist anything that forces double handling of salt or breaks ship rail access because this increases cost and emissions, undermining the tight logistics advantage Enstructure advertises for New Haven as a unique multimodal port location. 
[58]
Community and environmental advocates will rationally distrust “tarp plus housekeeping” regimes because the state record itself acknowledges enforcement history and because the permit’s detailed tarp specification implies that low grade covering is a known failure mode. 
[59]
The state’s permit structure is an intermediate position: it does not force domes for at scale facilities but it also increases the compliance burden and prescribes operational controls, which can raise cost enough that relocation becomes economically competitive if combined with grants and redevelopment value capture. 
[60]

Candidate site types and system design archetypes
The decision is best framed as choosing a system architecture rather than a single parcel because the primary risk is merely moving the problem from one shoreline to another.
Below is a schematic map of candidate site types and how they change the logistics and environmental profile.
Port import by ship or barge
         ↓
Option A  Waterfront enclosed terminal
         ↓ truck or rail distribution
Municipal buyers
Option B  Waterfront handling plus inland storage
Ship unload at port → short haul to inland covered shed → truck distribution
Option C  Inland only storage plus rail served import
Ship unload at alternate port or rail transload → inland rail served shed → truck distribution
Option D  Distributed smaller sheds plus brine model
Reduce peak inventory at waterfront by shifting to multiple municipal sheds and more brine use
Connecticut’s own permit siting constraints already create an objective screen for any new or relocated facility: avoid the 100 year floodplain, avoid 250 feet from potable wells, avoid Level A aquifer protection areas.

Funding sources that plausibly apply in New Haven
The most credible funding path in the retrieved record is to bundle salt relocation or redesign into port modernization and environmental mitigation programs because 
the operator and its partners are already receiving federal port related funding.
EPA Clean Ports Program
EPA states that Gateway Terminal in New Haven, in partnership with the New Haven Port Authority and Connecticut Port Authority, was selected to receive about $34 million for zero emission cargo handling equipment and related infrastructure, with explicit mention of community engagement and emissions reduction. This creates a funding and governance context where additional environmental mitigation commitments may be negotiable as part of a broader port community benefits strategy. 
[64]
US DOT Maritime Administration Port Infrastructure Development Program
Enstructure and partners received or were selected for an $11.2 million grant to add new rail yards and track in New Haven, indicating an existing federal investment thesis around cargo efficiency and reduced conflicts, which can be leveraged to justify moving bulk salt storage away from the Mill River waterfront while maintaining multimodal capabilities.

State and local revolving and water quality programs
New York municipal example shows NYS DEC Water Quality Improvement Project grants being used to fund a salt shed with local transportation funds as match, which suggests a relevant pattern even if Connecticut program specifics differ. 
[34]
Redevelopment value capture and lease restructuring
Southern Connecticut Gas Company’s remediation and leasing position implies that lease renegotiation, remediation scheduling, and land value changes could be part of a 
negotiated solution. The detectible risk is that if relocation is not coupled to a clear value proposition for the owner and operator, it becomes a pure cost center and will stall. 
[66]
Implementation roadmap and next steps
What to do first, because the data are currently insufficient for an investment grade decision
A decision about relocation versus rebuild requires an explicit baseline about how much salt is being stored, how often waves of exposure occur, and what the receiving water shows during storm events.
Step one: establish a factual baseline and public reporting framework
Require a site specific operational disclosure package that includes.
Peak on site inventory and seasonal inventory curve
Monthly receipts and load outs during the de icing season
Truck counts and peak hour activity
A map of stockpile footprint, impermeable pad extents, drainage inlets, outfalls, and any containment measures
This aligns with the Sector AE requirement to document seasonal timeframe and BMP and monitoring strategies in the SWPPP. 
[67]
Step two: implement independent inventory verification
Use quarterly LiDAR or drone photogrammetry surveys to generate independent volume estimates and reconcile against the operator’s reported inventories, borrowing directly from DOT inventory methods described in the recent LiDAR stockpile inventory paper. 
[16]
Step three: deploy a targeted environmental monitoring design
Monitor conductivity and chloride upstream and downstream and near shoreline edge during qualifying storm events, paired with sediment sampling where runoff contact is plausible. Connecticut DEEP’s own description of conductivity as a screening tool and chloride as confirmatory measurement provides the rationale, and the permit already requires stormwater sampling during qualifying storm events. 
[68]
Feasibility phase, focusing on options that preserve system functionality
A viable feasibility plan should produce a short list of two to three site type options plus a no build enhanced compliance option, each with conceptual engineering and permitting.
Option set recommended for New Haven specifically
Enhanced compliance at current site as a near term risk reduction baseline, because it is the only option that can be executed immediately under existing authority. This means full Sector AE compliance with tarp specs, sweeping, wheel wash, and strict minimization of uncovered time. 
[69]
Relocation within the port district but away from the Mill River edge, because it preserves ship rail truck advantages while reducing direct river adjacency and neighborhood exposure. This must be screened against floodplain and coastal permitting considerations and would likely require coordination with port authorities already engaged in rail yard improvements. 
[70]
Inland rail served covered storage yard linked to the port terminal by rail, because Enstructure’s own grant funded rail projects signal momentum and because it reduces truck concentration on Chapel Street while removing bulk storage from the river edge. 
[71]
A staged modular approach using a fabric structure at an interim inland site, because the Maine Saco case shows relocation can be executed quickly and at lower cost than replacement, and could be used to prove the logistics model before committing to a permanent shed. 
[36]
Permitting and approvals pathway, sequenced to avoid late stage failure
Sequence one: site screen and fatal flaw analysis
Apply the CT industrial stormwater permit siting constraints for new salt storage as an initial filter.
Reject sites in 100 year floodplain.
Reject sites within 250 feet of potable wells.
Reject sites within Level A aquifer protection areas.
This reduces wasted design effort. 
[28]
Sequence two: conceptual SWPPP and stormwater design
For each candidate, draft the SWPPP concept and stormwater system concept first, including how runoff is prevented from contacting salt, how any brine runoff is contained, and how operations keep pavement visible and clean. This should be aligned with Sector AE and with the permit’s focus on engineering certification for drainage system changes. 
[72]
Sequence three: land use and community process
Because the public record frames this as an Environmental Justice and waterfront land use conflict, treat community engagement as a gating requirement rather than an afterthought, leveraging the structure already used in the DEEP informational hearing process. 
[1]
Decision point and delivery roadmap
A realistic consulting style timeline is driven by winter seasonality. Salt handling changes should ideally be implemented outside peak winter operations.
Near term, next de icing season cycle
Implement enhanced compliance at the existing facility to reduce immediate risk: full tarp specification compliance, shorter uncovered windows, sweeping cadence, and wheel wash where feasible. 
[62]
Publish a baseline dashboard that includes inventories, uncovered time logs, and stormwater sampling summaries.
Medium term, site selection and conceptual design
Complete comparative feasibility of the three core architectures: current site upgrades, alternate waterfront within port district, inland rail served yard.
Identify candidate parcels and complete fatal flaw screening using floodplain and aquifer constraints already embedded in Connecticut’s permit approach. 
[29]
Long term, implementation and transition
Construct or retrofit covered storage and load out with containment.
Transition contracts and operations in a way that avoids winter supply disruption, including maintaining temporary inventory buffers.
